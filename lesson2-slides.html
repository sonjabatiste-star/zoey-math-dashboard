What Is a Dot Plot?

A dot plot shows how many times each number appears in a data set.

Example: Number of jumping jacks in 30 seconds:

5, 7, 7, 8, 10, 5, 6, 7

Each dot = 1 student. Numbers on the bottom = scores.

Ask Zoey:

  • “What does each dot represent?”
  • “What do the numbers on the bottom represent?”

Build a Dot Plot Together

Data: 5, 7, 7, 8, 10, 5, 6, 7

Draw a number line: 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10.

Then place dots:

  • Two dots above 5
  • One dot above 6
  • Three dots above 7
  • One dot above 8
  • One dot above 10

Ask Zoey:

  • “Which number has the most dots?”
  • “What does that tell us?”

Mode: The Most Popular Number

Mode = the number that appears the most.

From our data: 5, 7, 7, 8, 10, 5, 6, 7

  • 5 appears 2 times
  • 7 appears 3 times
  • 8, 10, 6 appear 1 time

Mode = 7

Ask Zoey: “Can a data set have more than one mode?”

Range: How Far Apart Are the Numbers?

Range = biggest number − smallest number.

Biggest = 10, Smallest = 5

Range = 10 − 5 = 5

Ask Zoey:

  • “What does the range tell us about how spread out the data is?”

Mean: The Fair-Share Idea

Mean is like a fair share.

Imagine each number is a stack of cubes. We “redistribute” cubes until all stacks are equal.

Ask Zoey:

  • “If we share the cubes fairly, what does each person get?”
  • “Why does that represent the mean?”

Calculate the Mean Together

Data: 5, 7, 7, 8, 10, 5, 6, 7

Step 1: Add them all:

5 + 7 + 7 + 8 + 10 + 5 + 6 + 7 = 55

Step 2: Count how many numbers: 8

Step 3: Divide:

Mean = 55 ÷ 8 ≈ 6.875 ≈ 7

Ask Zoey: “Does the mean match our fair-share idea?”
